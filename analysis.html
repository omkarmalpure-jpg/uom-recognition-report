Deep Dive — How Step 5 Works and Why It Succeeds

Part 1 — DBSCAN-Built Thresholds (ThresholdV2)

Why the old thresholds (V1) were not enough

Steps 1–4 rely on hand-crafted or statistically naive min/max price ranges. A single extreme purchase can widen a V1 threshold so much that it matches too broadly — or, when data is sparse, the range is too tight to match realistic new transactions.

What DBSCAN does differently

DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) groups historical purchase prices for each product into dense clusters while treating statistical outliers as noise. The resulting ThresholdV2 records store:

  • MinThreshold / MaxThreshold — the actual observed price range of the cluster (not a hand-tuned rule).
  • QuantityOfEAInUOM — the UOM quantity that cluster represents.
  • MeanEA — a per-each price anchor used when no WAP entry is available.

Because outliers are excluded, the clusters are tighter and more representative of real analyst expectations. A product that historically sells as a BX/12 at $120–$180 will produce one cluster with that range; a separate cluster covers the EA sales at $10–$15.

Part 2 — The Proximity Inference Formula

Once DBSCAN clusters exist, Step 5 must decide which cluster (UOM) best explains the current line’s PriceUOM. The key insight is that price operates on a log scale — a $5 difference matters much more at a $10 product than at a $10,000 product.

Log-distance calculation

logPrice = log₁₀(max(PriceUOM, 0.0001))
logLo    = log₁₀(max(MinThreshold, 0.0001))
logHi    = log₁₀(max(MaxThreshold, 0.0001))

logDist  = 0                        if  lo ≤ price ≤ hi
         = logPrice − logHi         if  price > hi   (above range)
         = logLo − logPrice         if  price < lo   (below range)

If the price falls inside the cluster band, the log-distance is exactly 0 — a perfect match. Outside the band the distance grows as the log ratio between the price and the nearest edge.

Gaussian proximity score

proximityScore = exp(−0.5 × (logDist / LOG_EPS)²)    where LOG_EPS = 0.25

This is a Gaussian kernel on log-price space. LOG_EPS = 0.25 corresponds to roughly a 1.8× price deviation from the cluster edge before the score drops to ~0.37. At a 2× deviation the score is ~0.02 — effectively zero.

Why this mirrors analyst reasoning:

Analysts verify a UOM assignment by mentally checking whether the price is “in the right ballpark” for that product and pack size. A box of 10 syringes should cost roughly 10× what one syringe costs. If the file price is 3× or 20× off, the analyst rejects it. The proximity score formalises this intuition: prices within the cluster score near 1.0, prices slightly outside drop gracefully, and wildly mismatched prices score near 0.

Four candidate pools

Step 5 evaluates up to four candidate sources and picks the one with the highest proximity score:

Source What it is Why it’s included
DBSCAN Cluster from ThresholdV2 Primary signal — density-derived from history
Known Product UOM WAP (Weighted Avg Price) from purchase history Covers UOMs not yet in DBSCAN clusters
EA/1 WAP Per-each derived from any known WAP ÷ quantity Catches single-unit sales missing from DBSCAN
File UOM × WAP File-stated UOM quantity × per-each WAP Validates when the supplier already labelled the pack size

The File UOM candidate requires a minimum score of 0.85 to be considered — a stricter bar because file labels are not always reliable.

Winner-Source Breakdown

Candidate Recognitions % of Step 5 total
DBSCAN cluster 47,554 93.6 %

EA/1 WAP candidate 1,128 2.2 %

DBSCAN is the dominant winner (93.6 %), validating that the density-based clusters are the most reliable signal. The remaining 6.4 % are split across the three supplementary pools — each of which catches a class of products the primary DBSCAN clusters do not yet cover.

Why Unrecognized Rows Stay Unrecognized

Three root causes account for nearly all unrecognized rows:

1. No usable PriceUOM (5.9 % of unrecognized)

Step 5 requires a PriceUOM to compute a log-distance. Rows where PriceUOM is null or zero (3,168 rows) are immediately ineligible.

2. Low or zero purchase history

Purchase history Unrecognized rows % of total unrecognized
Zero (no history at all) 47 0.1 %
Very low (1–4 units) 31,400 58.4 %
Some history (≥ 5 units) 21,900 40.7 %
  • Zero history — DBSCAN cannot form a cluster without past transactions. No ThresholdV2 exists, and no WAP can be derived. Step 5 has nothing to anchor its proximity calculation against.
  • Very low history (1–4 units) — a single purchase creates a technically valid cluster, but with such sparse data the cluster edges may be wildly unrepresentative. The proximity score lands below the 0.30 acceptance threshold.
  • Some history but low confidence — the product has enough data for DBSCAN, but the file’s PriceUOM is far enough from every cluster that no candidate scores ≥ 0.30. This typically means the supplier is quoting an unusual pack size not yet seen in history, or there is a unit-price vs pack-price mismatch in the file.

3. PriceUOM not present in purchase history

Some rows have a PriceUOM value but that price point has never appeared in the product’s purchase history. With no matching cluster and no comparable WAP anchor, the inference step cannot form a confident assignment — the proximity score for every candidate falls below 0.30 and the row exits unrecognized.
